Seven Principles of Typographic Contrast

In typography, as in music, harmony and contrast are the keys to composition.
Written by John D. Berry, based on the teachings of Carl Dair

Harmony and Contrast

The late Canadian typographer Carl Dair was one of the great typographic designers of the 1950s and 1960s, known for his ability to explain and demonstrate the expressive power of type. His book Design With Type remains a foundational guide for designers who seek to make type not just readable, but meaningful.

Dair believed that the essence of design lies in the deliberate play between harmony and contrast — rhythm and emphasis. Typography, like music, depends on variation, tempo, and structure to create meaning and beauty.

Seven Kinds of Contrast

In his fifth booklet of A Typographic Quest, Carl Dair identifies seven key types of typographic contrast that bring life and clarity to design. Each principle is a way to make type speak more powerfully and distinctly.

1. Contrast of Size

A simple but dramatic change in size draws attention and establishes hierarchy. Large titles paired with smaller text create rhythm and focus, guiding the reader through the composition.

2. Contrast of Weight

Bold type stands out among lighter text, establishing visual anchors. This principle also applies to other visual elements — lines, rules, or dots — that can emphasize certain parts of the layout.

3. Contrast of Form

Differences between roman and italic type, or between uppercase and lowercase letters, create dynamic tension. Dair warned, however, against mixing scripts and italics together since both mimic handwriting and may conflict rather than contrast.

4. Contrast of Structure

This involves using distinctly different typeface families — for example, pairing a monoline sans serif with a high-contrast modern serif. The goal is not just variation, but complementary distinction.

5. Contrast of Texture

Typography has texture — the overall pattern of light and dark formed by text on a page. Adjusting line spacing, letter spacing, and typeface combinations affects how this “fabric of communication” feels and reads.

6. Contrast of Color

Color contrast influences tone and hierarchy. Even a shift from black to dark gray can change the reader’s perception of importance. Always consider tonal value and background when applying color.

7. Contrast of Direction

Directional contrast plays with alignment and angle — vertical versus horizontal, or slight rotation. A single diagonal element can bring energy and motion to an otherwise static layout.

Other Types of Contrast

Dair also mentions contrast by isolation, where an element stands apart from others to capture attention. Non-typographic contrasts — like paper texture or ink finish — also contribute to a composition’s tactile and visual rhythm.

Ultimately, Dair’s ideas teach us to be intentional: contrast should always be strong and purposeful. Subtle differences often fail to communicate; strong contrast creates clarity and rhythm.
